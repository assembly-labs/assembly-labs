A word on offshore development.

Your bids from offshore development shops may be at prices that seem too good to pass up. I've worked with offshore teams myself early in my career. Some are "okay" for simple websites. When it comes to software for businesses, they fail. "Good money after bad" is common. The past two years have been a shift back to US-based teams since the calculus has fundamentally changed—and for a health and wellness business handling sensitive customer data, the risks deserve a clear-eyed look.

What is technical debt?

Technical debt is what accumulates when developers take shortcuts to meet deadlines. It's the difference between code that works today and code that's maintainable tomorrow. Offshore teams, often under pressure to deliver quickly at low cost, frequently skip testing, documentation, and proper architecture. The result: your product becomes slower to evolve, bugs multiply, and eventually you're paying American developers premium rates just to understand and fix what was built cheaply overseas. Studies show that fixing issues post-launch can cost up to 100x more than addressing them during development.

The accountability gap

When something goes wrong with an offshore team, you have limited recourse. Different time zones mean delays in communication. Cultural and language barriers create misunderstandings that compound over time. And when the project ends, you're often left with minimal documentation, creating a dependency on a team that may have already moved on. As one analysis put it: "The hidden challenges—technical debt, communication friction, security risks, and weak ownership—can silently drain resources and undermine product quality long before anyone notices."

Data security: A real concern for wellness businesses

This is where it gets serious. dtXfy will handle HSA/FSA payments, membership data, and potentially HIPAA-adjacent health information. Offshore call centers and development shops have been at the center of major data breaches where customer information was actively sold.

Coinbase, 2024–2025: Offshore support agents at TaskUs in India were bribed $200 per screenshot to capture and sell customer data. Over 69,000 users were affected. Estimated losses: $180–400 million. 226 employees were terminated. Arrests followed in India.

BSNL Telecom, 2024: A breach at India's state-owned telecom compromised millions of users' data, which surfaced for sale on dark web marketplaces shortly after.

The Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre reported an average of 7,000 cybercrime complaints per day in early 2024. This isn't fear-mongering—it's the operating environment. When your software handles customer PII and payment data, the provenance of your development team matters.
